Introduction

The hotel industry plays a crucial role in the global economy, yet it continuously faces challenges related to fluctuating demand and high cancellation rates. Understanding the key drivers of booking trends and cancellations is essential for optimizing revenue management, improving customer satisfaction, and enhancing operational efficiency. This study aims to analyze the critical factors influencing hotel booking cancellations, including:

  • Seasonality & Weather Fluctuations â€“ Peak seasons boost bookings, while adverse weather leads to higher cancellations.

  • Pricing Strategies & Discounts â€“ Competitive pricing, early-bird offers, and dynamic rates influence booking commitment.

  • Booking Platforms & Policies â€“ Online travel agencies (OTAs) provide flexible cancellations, whereas direct hotel bookings enforce stricter rules.

  • Customer Segmentation â€“ Business travelerâ€™s book with certainty, while leisure travelers show more cancellation tendencies.

  • Economic Shifts & Market Trends â€“ Recessions, inflation, and exchange rates impact global and local travel demand.

  • Hotel Reputation & Digital Presence â€“ Online reviews and brand loyalty strongly dictate customer trust and booking rates.

  • Unpredictable Disruptions â€“ Pandemics, natural disasters, and strikes trigger large-scale cancellations.

  • Cancellation Policies & Refund Options â€“ Non-refundable rates lower cancellations but deter risk-averse travelers.

  • AI & Smart Booking Technologies â€“ Machine learning-based pricing and chatbot engagement shape modern booking behaviors.

By examining these factors, this study seeks to provide data-driven insights that can help hotels refine pricing strategies, optimize occupancy rates, and mitigate revenue losses due to cancellations.

The data covers hotel bookings from July 1st, 2015, to August 31st, 2017, providing insights into booking cancellations, lead times, average daily rates, holidays, and guest counts.

Data Cleaning and Preparation

Variables included:

  • is_canceled: Binary target variable indicating booking cancellation (1 = Canceled, 0 = Not Canceled).

  • hotel: Categorical variable for hotel type (City Hotel or Resort Hotel).

  • lead_time: Numerical variable indicating the number of days between booking and arrival.

  • adr (Average Daily Rate): Numerical variable representing the daily room rate.

  • is_holiday: Binary variable indicating if the booking date is a holiday.

  • guests: Numerical variable representing the total number of guests.

  • arrival_date_month: Categorical variable to capture seasonal trends.

Variables excluded:

  • agent and company: High percentage of missing values and minimal impact on cancellation predictions.

  • reservation_status: Perfectly correlated with the target variable (is_canceled), leading to data leakage.

  • country: High cardinality with minimal influence on cancellation behavior.

  • children and babies: Combined into the guestâ€™s variable for simplicity.

  • arrival_date_day_of_month: Excluded as the month variable sufficiently captures seasonality.

Preparation:

  • calendar_dates: Created by combining arrival_date_month and arrival_date_day_of_month to facilitate holiday matching.

  • is_holiday: A binary variable indicating whether a booking date is a holiday

  • guests: Total number of guests calculated by summing adults, children, and babies

  • hotel and customer_type were converted into factors for modeling.

  • is_holiday was encoded as a binary variable (0 = Normal Day, 1 = Holiday).

  • For the guests variable, missing values were filled with 0

The dependent variable, is_canceled, is a binary variable indicating whether a booking was canceled (1) or not (0). The independent variables included hotel type (City Hotel vs.Â Resort Hotel), lead_time (days between booking and arrival), adr (average daily rate), is_holiday (indicator for holiday period), and guests (number of guests).

Results

Linear Probability Model

The LPM results indicate that lead_time is a strong predictor of cancellations (p < 0.001), suggesting that bookings made further in advance are more likely to be canceled. The coefficient for hotel type is negative, implying that bookings at Resort Hotels are less likely to be canceled compared to City Hotels. The adr variable has a positive coefficient, suggesting that higher average daily rates are associated with a slightly increased likelihood of cancellation. The is_holiday variable has a small but significant negative effect, indicating that bookings during holidays are slightly less likely to be canceled. The number of guests is positively associated with cancellation likelihood. The model has an adjusted R^2 value of 0.1022, indicating that approximately 10.2% of the variation in cancellations is explained by the model.

Logistic Regression Model of All Hotels

The logistic regression model produces coefficient estimates that are similar in magnitude and statistical significance to those from the Linear Probability Model (LPM), reinforcing the consistency and reliability of the findings. The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) value of 144,895 suggests an adequate model fit, while the number of iterations (2) indicates that the model converged efficiently.

Logistic Regression Model of City Hotels

The AIC value of 99,870 for the City Hotel-specific model suggests a better fit compared to the all-hotels model, indicating that modeling City Hotel cancellations separately may improve predictive accuracy.

Discussion and Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that lead_time is the strongest predictor of hotel booking cancellations. The logistic regression results indicate that for each additional day in lead time, the odds of cancellation increase by 1.006 times (exp(0.0059) â‰ˆ 1.006), holding everything else constant.

The Logistic Regression model outperforms the Linear Probability Model (LPM) since the dependent variable is binary. While LPM provides an interpretable way to assess relationships, it does not constrain predicted probabilities between 0 and 1, making it unsuitable for classification. Additionally, heteroskedasticity is a common issue in LPMs, leading to inefficient estimates.

Although the logistic regression model captures statistically significant predictors, the McFaddenâ€™s Pseudo RÂ² (0.0795) in the first logistic model suggests that additional factors influence cancellations beyond those included in the model. Incorporating variables related to customer behavior, economic conditions, and competitor pricing could improve predictive accuracy.

While the model achieves high specificity (88.1%), meaning it effectively identifies bookings that will not be canceled, it struggles with low sensitivity (30.7%), indicating that it fails to detect many actual cancellations. This misclassification could lead to revenue loss, as hotels may not properly manage inventory to account for expected cancellations. Adjusting the classification threshold or incorporating additional predictive features may improve sensitivity.

The results confirm that Resort Hotels have lower cancellation rates than City Hotels. Since City Hotels experience more cancellations, we expected the City Hotel-specific model to outperform the all-hotels model. While it did improve sensitivity (38.4% vs.Â 30.7%), the overall improvement in accuracy was marginal.
